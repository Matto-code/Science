Big picture — what is this topic about?

A force is a push or a pull. Forces can change the speed, direction or shape of an object. This topic is all about understanding how forces act on objects and how they affect motion.

The curriculum (AC9S7U04) says you need to be able to:

  • Identify and describe different types of forces (contact and non-contact)
  • Represent forces using diagrams with arrows
  • Explain the difference between balanced and unbalanced forces
  • Calculate net force and determine its direction
  • Explain gravity and the difference between mass and weight
  • Relate changes in motion to mass, magnitude and direction of forces

Key concepts at a glance

Force
A push or pull that can change an object's speed, direction or shape. Measured in Newtons (N).
Balanced forces
Forces that are equal and opposite. The object does NOT change its motion.
Unbalanced forces
Forces that are NOT equal. The object DOES change its motion — it speeds up, slows down or changes direction.
Net force
The overall (resultant) force after all forces are added up. Determines what actually happens to the object.

Contact forces vs non-contact forces

Contact forces require physical contact between objects — they must be touching.

Examples: friction, normal force, tension, applied force, air resistance
Non-contact forces act at a distance — the objects do NOT need to touch.

Examples: gravity, magnetic force, electrostatic force
A magnet can pull a paperclip from across a gap — that's a non-contact force. Your foot kicking a ball requires contact — that's a contact force.

Common types of forces — tap each to learn more

🔴 Friction Contact
Opposes motion between surfaces in contact. Slows things down.
🟦 Normal force Contact
A surface pushing back on an object resting on it. Always perpendicular to the surface.
🟢 Tension Contact
Force transmitted through a string, rope or cable when pulled tight.
🟡 Applied force Contact
A force applied directly to an object by a person or another object — pushing or pulling.
💨 Air resistance Contact
A type of friction between an object and the air. Opposes motion through air.
🌍 Gravity Non-contact
An attractive force between all objects with mass. Pulls objects toward Earth's centre.
🧲 Magnetic Non-contact
Force between magnets and magnetic materials. Can attract or repel at a distance.
⚡ Electrostatic Non-contact
Force between electrically charged objects. Can attract or repel at a distance.

Everyday examples — which type of force?

Sliding a box across the floor → Applied force (pushing) + Friction (opposing) — both Contact
A ball falling from a tree → Gravity — Non-contact
A book resting on a table → Gravity down + Normal force up — both acting on the book
Paperclip pulled by magnet → Magnetic force — Non-contact
Parachutist falling → Gravity down + Air resistance up — Non-contact + Contact
A balloon sticking to a wall after rubbing → Electrostatic force — Non-contact

Balanced forces

Balanced forces occur when all forces acting on an object cancel each other out. The net force = 0 N.

Result: the object does NOT change its motion — it stays still, or keeps moving at the same speed in the same direction.
Balanced ≠ no forces. A book on a table has gravity pulling down AND a normal force pushing up. They balance — net force is zero, so the book doesn't move.

Examples of balanced forces

Book on a table: Gravity (10 N down) + Normal force (10 N up) = 0 N net force. Book stays still. ✓
Car at constant speed: Engine force forward = Air resistance + friction backward. Net force = 0. Speed stays the same. ✓
Tug-of-war tie: Team A pulls left 500 N, Team B pulls right 500 N. Net force = 0. Nobody moves. ✓

Unbalanced forces

Unbalanced forces occur when forces do NOT cancel out. The net force ≠ 0 N.

Result: the object changes its motion — it speeds up, slows down, or changes direction (accelerates).

Examples of unbalanced forces

Kicking a ball: Your foot applies a large force forward. Ground friction is small. Net force is forward → ball accelerates forward. ✓
Falling apple: Gravity pulls down 5 N. Air resistance pushes up 0.1 N. Net force = 4.9 N downward → apple accelerates down. ✓
Braking car: Brakes apply friction. Engine force reduces. Net force is backward → car slows down. ✓

Calculating net force

Net force is the total force on an object after all forces are combined. Forces in the same direction are added. Forces in opposite directions are subtracted.
Same direction: Net force = Force 1 + Force 2
Opposite directions: Net force = Larger force − Smaller force (in direction of larger force)
Two people push a box: Person A pushes right with 30 N, Person B pushes right with 20 N. What is the net force? ▼
1
Both forces are in the same direction (right), so add them.
2
Net force = 30 + 20 = 50 N to the right
✓ Net force = 50 N to the right → box accelerates right
A tug-of-war: Team A pulls left with 400 N, Team B pulls right with 350 N. What is the net force? ▼
1
Forces are in opposite directions — subtract the smaller from the larger.
2
Net force = 400 − 350 = 50 N
3
Direction = direction of the larger force (left, Team A).
✓ Net force = 50 N to the left → rope moves toward Team A
A car engine pushes forward with 3000 N. Air resistance and friction together push backward with 3000 N. What happens? ▼
1
Net force = 3000 − 3000 = 0 N
2
Net force is zero → forces are balanced
✓ Net force = 0 N → car moves at constant speed (no change in motion)

Gravity

Gravity is a non-contact attractive force that pulls all objects with mass toward each other. On Earth, gravity pulls everything toward the centre of the Earth.
  • Gravity acts on ALL objects with mass — there are no exceptions
  • The more mass an object has, the stronger the gravitational force on it
  • Earth's gravitational field strength is approximately 10 N/kg (or 9.8 N/kg more precisely)
  • Gravity is always directed downward (toward Earth's centre)

Mass vs Weight — a critical distinction

This is one of the most commonly confused concepts in Year 7 physics. Mass and weight are NOT the same thing.
Mass
• The amount of matter in an object
• Measured in kilograms (kg)
• Stays the SAME everywhere
• Your mass on the Moon = your mass on Earth
• Not a force
Weight
• The gravitational force pulling an object down
• Measured in Newtons (N)
• CHANGES depending on gravity
• Your weight on the Moon is 1/6 of Earth weight
• IS a force
In everyday life we say "I weigh 60 kg" — but in science, 60 kg is your mass. Your weight would be about 600 N on Earth.

Calculating weight

W = m × g
Weight (N) = mass (kg) × gravitational field strength (N/kg)
On Earth: g = 10 N/kg
A student has a mass of 50 kg. What is their weight on Earth (g = 10 N/kg)? ▼
1
Write the formula: W = m × g
2
Substitute: W = 50 × 10
3
Calculate: W = 500 N
✓ Weight = 500 N downward
A student weighs 600 N on Earth. What is their mass? ▼
1
Formula: W = m × g, rearrange for mass: m = W ÷ g
2
Substitute: m = 600 ÷ 10
3
Calculate: m = 60 kg
✓ Mass = 60 kg
A 60 kg astronaut travels to the Moon where g = 1.6 N/kg. What is their weight on the Moon? ▼
1
Formula: W = m × g
2
Substitute Moon's g: W = 60 × 1.6
3
Calculate: W = 96 N
✓ Weight on Moon = 96 N (compared to 600 N on Earth)
Mass stays 60 kg everywhere — only weight changes!

How to draw force diagrams

Force diagrams (also called free body diagrams) use arrows to show all forces acting on an object.
  • Draw the object as a simple box or dot in the centre
  • Draw an arrow for EACH force acting on the object
  • The direction of the arrow shows the direction of the force
  • The length of the arrow shows the size (magnitude) of the force — bigger force = longer arrow
  • Label each arrow with the name and value of the force (e.g. "Gravity 50 N")
Only draw forces acting ON the object — not forces the object exerts on other things.

How forces affect motion

A force only changes motion when it is unbalanced. The three things that determine what happens to an object are: the magnitude (size) of the net force, the direction of the net force, and the mass of the object.

Effect of magnitude (size of force)

A larger net force causes a greater change in motion (greater acceleration). If you push a shopping trolley harder, it speeds up faster.

Effect of direction

The object accelerates in the same direction as the net force. Push right → moves right. Push up → moves up.

Effect of mass

A larger mass means less change in motion for the same force. It's harder to push a fully loaded trolley than an empty one. The same force produces less acceleration for a greater mass.

Mass and motion — the relationship

This is Newton's Second Law of Motion — you don't need to know the law by name at Year 7, but you need to understand the relationship.
For the same force: Double the mass → half the acceleration (harder to move)
For the same mass: Double the force → double the acceleration (faster change)

Worked examples

Two boxes are pushed with the same force. Box A has mass 5 kg, Box B has mass 20 kg. Which accelerates more? ▼
1
Same force is applied to both boxes.
2
Box A has less mass → less resistance to force → greater acceleration.
3
Box B has more mass → more resistance to force → less acceleration.
✓ Box A (5 kg) accelerates more. Box B (20 kg) accelerates less for the same force.
A net force of 20 N acts on a 2 kg ball. What happens if the same 20 N net force acts on a 10 kg ball? ▼
1
Same force (20 N), but mass increases from 2 kg to 10 kg — 5 times more mass.
2
Greater mass → harder to accelerate → acceleration is 5 times smaller.
✓ The 10 kg ball accelerates much less than the 2 kg ball for the same force.
Mass resists changes in motion — this resistance is called inertia.

Summary — what causes what?

Balanced forces (net force = 0 N) → Object stays still OR continues moving at the same speed in the same direction. No change in motion.
Unbalanced forces (net force ≠ 0 N) → Object changes its motion — speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.
Larger force → greater change in motion (more acceleration)
Larger mass → less change in motion (less acceleration) for the same force
Direction of net force → determines the direction of the change in motion

On Earth: g = 10 N/kg
Balanced forces: net force = 0 N → no change in motion
Unbalanced forces: net force ≠ 0 N → object accelerates in direction of net force
Contact forces: friction, normal, tension, applied, air resistance
Non-contact forces: gravity, magnetic, electrostatic
