Contact vs non-contact forces

Contact forces require physical contact — objects must be touching.

Examples: friction, normal force, tension, applied force, air resistance, upthrust
Non-contact forces act at a distance — objects do NOT need to touch.

Examples: gravity, magnetic force, electrostatic force

Measuring force — the spring balance

A spring balance (Newton meter) measures force in Newtons (N). It works by stretching a spring — the more force, the more it stretches, the higher the reading on the scale.

Air resistance — exam detail

How air resistance works: As an object moves through air, it collides with air molecules. These collisions push back against the object, creating a force that opposes its motion.
Why it increases with speed: At higher speeds, the object hits more air molecules per second and each collision is more forceful. Faster = more collisions = more air resistance.

Balanced forces

Balanced forces are equal in size and opposite in direction. Net force = 0 N.

Result: no change in motion — stays still OR continues at constant speed.
A resultant force of 0 N means two things are possible: the object is stationary, OR it's moving at constant speed in a straight line.
Book on table: Gravity 10 N down, Normal 10 N up → balanced → still ✓
Car at constant speed: Engine forward = friction + air resistance backward → balanced → constant speed ✓

Unbalanced forces

Unbalanced forces do NOT cancel out. Net force ≠ 0 N.

Result: the object changes its motion — speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.
Kicking a ball: Foot force > friction → net force forward → ball accelerates ✓
Tug-of-war (one team stronger): Unequal forces → rope accelerates toward the stronger team ✓

Terminal velocity

Free fall (early): Gravity (large) > Air resistance (small) → unbalanced → skydiver accelerates downward.
Terminal velocity: As speed increases, air resistance grows until it equals gravity. Forces become balanced → net force = 0 → skydiver falls at constant speed.
In force diagrams: free fall has unequal arrows (gravity longer). Terminal velocity has equal-length arrows (gravity = air resistance).

Calculating net force

Same direction: Add the forces. Opposite directions: Subtract smaller from larger (net force in direction of larger).
60 N right, 15 N left. Net force? ▼
1
Opposite → subtract: 60 − 15 = 45 N
✓ Net force = 45 N to the right (direction of larger force)
200 N right, 50 N left. Net force? ▼
1
Opposite → subtract: 200 − 50 = 150 N
✓ Net force = 150 N to the right
200 N right, 40 N right (same direction). Net force? ▼
1
Same direction → add: 200 + 40 = 240 N
✓ Net force = 240 N to the right
300 N left, 190 N right. Net force? ▼
1
Opposite → subtract: 300 − 190 = 110 N
✓ Net force = 110 N to the left (direction of larger)
2500 N up, 2500 N down. What happens? ▼
1
Equal and opposite: 2500 − 2500 = 0 N
✓ Balanced → no change in motion (stays still or constant speed)

Mass vs Weight

Mass
Amount of matter
Measured in kg
Stays the same everywhere
Not a force
Weight
Gravitational force
Measured in Newtons (N)
Changes with gravity
IS a force
W = m × g
Weight (N) = Mass (kg) × Gravitational field strength (N/kg)  |  Earth: g = 10 N/kg
A student has mass 50 kg. Weight on Earth? ▼
1
W = 50 × 10 = 500 N
✓ Weight = 500 N downward
Object weighs 600 N on Earth. Mass? ▼
1
m = W ÷ g = 600 ÷ 10 = 60 kg
✓ Mass = 60 kg (stays 60 kg everywhere)
60 kg astronaut on the Moon (g = 1.6 N/kg). Weight? ▼
1
W = 60 × 1.6 = 96 N (vs 600 N on Earth)
✓ Weight = 96 N. Mass stays 60 kg.

How to draw force diagrams

Arrows show: Direction (which way) and Magnitude (how big — longer arrow = bigger force). Label each arrow with the force name and value.
Only draw forces acting ON the object — not forces the object exerts on other things.

If D is thrust (forward), what is B (backward)?

Drag (air resistance) — it always opposes the direction of motion. If thrust pushes forward, drag pushes backward.

Speed, Distance and Time

Speed = Distance ÷ Time
S = D/T  |  D = S × T  |  T = D/S
The formula triangle: Put D (distance) on top, S (speed) and T (time) on the bottom. Cover the one you want to find — the remaining two show the calculation.
Units: Speed in m/s or km/h. Distance in m or km. Time in seconds or hours. Make sure units match!
If distance is in km and time in hours → speed is km/h. If distance is in m and time in seconds → speed is m/s. Watch for unit traps (e.g. time given in minutes — convert to seconds or hours first).

Worked examples from your revision sheet

A car travels 5 km in 3 hours. Calculate its speed. ▼
1
S = D ÷ T = 5 ÷ 3 = 1.67 km/h
✓ Speed = 1.67 km/h
A horse walks at 6 km/h. How far will it walk in 2.5 hours? ▼
1
D = S × T = 6 × 2.5 = 15 km
✓ Distance = 15 km
Terminal velocity 90 m/s, fallen 800 m at that speed. How long? ▼
1
T = D ÷ S = 800 ÷ 90 = 8.89 seconds
✓ Time ≈ 8.9 seconds
A car travels 4 km in 5 minutes. Average speed? ▼
1
Convert: 5 min = 5 ÷ 60 = 1/12 hour
2
S = D ÷ T = 4 ÷ (1/12) = 4 × 12 = 48 km/h
✓ Speed = 48 km/h
OR in m/s: 4000 m ÷ 300 s = 13.3 m/s
A plane: 7000 km in 8 hours. Speed? ▼
1
S = D ÷ T = 7000 ÷ 8 = 875 km/h
✓ Speed = 875 km/h
Chicken runs 40 m in 10 seconds. Speed? ▼
1
S = D ÷ T = 40 ÷ 10 = 4 m/s
✓ Speed = 4 m/s
Sloth climbs 15 m tree in 10 minutes. Speed? ▼
1
Convert: 10 min = 600 seconds
2
S = D ÷ T = 15 ÷ 600 = 0.025 m/s

Parts of a lever

Every lever has 3 parts:
1. Effort (Force) — the force you apply
2. Load — the object being moved
3. Fulcrum (Pivot) — the fixed point the lever rotates around

Three classes of levers

1st Class Lever
Load — FULCRUM — Effort
Fulcrum is in the MIDDLE (between effort and load)
Examples: seesaw, scissors, pliers, crowbar, claw hammer
2nd Class Lever
Fulcrum — LOAD — Effort
Load is in the MIDDLE (between fulcrum and effort)
Examples: wheelbarrow, nutcracker, bottle opener, stapler, door
3rd Class Lever
Fulcrum — EFFORT — Load
Effort is in the MIDDLE (between fulcrum and load)
Examples: fishing rod, tweezers/tongs, broom, shovel, human arm
Memory trick: think about what's in the middle. 1st = F(ulcrum). 2nd = L(oad). 3rd = E(ffort). FLE — say "FLEE" to remember the order!

Classify these levers (from your revision sheet)

Fishing rod — Fulcrum (handle) → Effort (your hand) → Load (fish) → 3rd class
Stapler — Fulcrum (hinge at back) → Load (staple) → Effort (press front) → 2nd class
Scissors — Effort (handles) → Fulcrum (pivot) → Load (paper) → 1st class
Wheelbarrow — Fulcrum (wheel) → Load (tray) → Effort (handles) → 2nd class
Seesaw — Load → Fulcrum (centre pivot) → Effort → 1st class
Tweezers/tongs — Fulcrum (joined end) → Effort (squeeze middle) → Load (tips) → 3rd class
Claw hammer — Effort (handle) → Fulcrum (head edge) → Load (nail) → 1st class
Shovel — Fulcrum (lower hand) → Effort (upper hand) → Load (dirt at end) → 3rd class
Human arm — Fulcrum (elbow) → Effort (bicep) → Load (hand/object) → 3rd class

Inertia

Inertia is an object's resistance to change in its motion. An object at rest stays at rest; an object in motion stays in motion — unless acted on by an unbalanced force.
More mass = more inertia = harder to start, stop or change direction. That's why a full trolley is harder to push around a corner than an empty one.

How force, mass and direction affect motion

Larger force → greater change in motion (more acceleration)
Larger mass → less change in motion (less acceleration) for same force
Direction of net force → object accelerates in the same direction as the net force
Balanced forces (0 N) → no change — stays still or constant speed
Unbalanced forces (≠ 0 N) → speeds up, slows down, or changes direction

Formulas to know

Weight: W = m × g  (Earth: g = 10 N/kg)
Speed: S = D ÷ T  |  D = S × T  |  T = D ÷ S
Force: F = m × a  |  a = F ÷ m  |  m = F ÷ a
Net force same dir: Add  |  Opposite dir: Subtract (direction of larger)
Lever classes (FLE): 1st = Fulcrum middle  |  2nd = Load middle  |  3rd = Effort middle
